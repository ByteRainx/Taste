# The Concept Behind "Research Taste"

> "The great scientists have tremendous taste in picking problems." — Richard Hamming

## Why Taste Matters

Academia has plenty of metrics: h-index, citation count, impact factor, publication count. These measure **output** and **attention**. None of them measure **taste**.

Yet when researchers talk about each other, taste is the first thing they mention:

- "She has great taste in problems — always picks the right thing to work on."
- "His methods are always so clean and elegant."
- "They always seem to be two years ahead of the field."

Taste is what separates a researcher who publishes 100 incremental papers from one who publishes 20 papers that reshape a field.

## What Makes This Project Different

| Existing Tools | What They Measure | What They Miss |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Google Scholar | Citation count, h-index | Why people cite them |
| Semantic Scholar | Citation graph, topics | How they think |
| AMiner | Collaboration network | What makes them unique |
| Connected Papers | Related paper graph | The "signature" in their work |

**taste** is the first tool that analyzes the _content_ of papers to extract _qualitative_ patterns about _how_ a researcher does research.

## The "Paper is cheap, show me the taste" Philosophy

Inspired by Linus Torvalds' famous "Talk is cheap, show me the code":

- **Talk is cheap** → anyone can talk about ideas
- **Paper is cheap** → anyone can publish papers (especially now)
- **Show me the code** → the code reveals the real engineering quality
- **Show me the taste** → the papers reveal the real research quality

In an era of LLM-assisted writing and paper mills, the raw number of publications means less and less. What matters is the _pattern_ — the consistent, distinctive way a researcher approaches their craft.

- **Science journalists** profiling researchers → richer narratives than "X published in Nature"
- **Anyone curious** about what makes great researchers great

## Example: Kaiming He's Taste

Even without running the tool, most CV researchers would describe Kaiming He's taste as:

- **Problem**: Asks "why doesn't X work?" rather than "how to make X better?"
- **Method**: Absurdly simple solutions (one skip connection, mask 75% of patches)
- **Aesthetic**: Clean, minimal, iconic naming (ResNet, MAE)
- **Timing**: Enters fields at the right moment (deep learning for detection in 2014, self-supervised in 2019)
- **Evolution**: Image processing → detection → recognition → self-supervised, each transition perfectly timed

This "taste profile" tells you more about Kaiming He as a researcher than any metric could.

**taste** automates and systematizes this kind of analysis.
